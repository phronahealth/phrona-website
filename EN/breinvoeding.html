Brain and nutrition

Updated: April 2026

Our brain accounts for roughly 2% of our body weight but consumes around 20% of our total energy needs. What we eat therefore directly influences how the brain functions: concentration, mood, memory and appetite. The relationship between nutrition and brain function is more complex than often assumed, driven by an interplay of hormones, neurotransmitters and evolutionary mechanisms.

How the brain steers food choices

The primary function of food is to supply fuel and building blocks. When the stomach empties, ghrelin — the 'hunger hormone' — rises and activates brain regions that drive motivation and reward behaviour (Cummings et al., 2002). That hunger is not a weakness or lack of willpower: it is a strongly conserved survival mechanism.

Berthoud (2011) describes how two systems in the brain are constantly interacting: the homeostatic system (hypothalamus) regulates energy balance based on physiological needs, while the hedonic system (primarily the reward circuit) responds to taste, smell and the anticipation of pleasure. In environments of food abundance, the hedonic system regularly 'wins' — even when we objectively need no energy.

Dopamine and food reward

Foods rich in fat, sugar and salt activate the dopaminergic reward system more strongly than unprocessed foods (Volkow et al., 2011). Dopamine is released both in anticipation of and during consumption of palatable food, reinforcing habitual behaviour: the more often a particular food is eaten in a particular context, the stronger the drive to repeat it.

This mechanism explains why ultra-processed food — engineered to maximise precisely those combinations of fat, sugar and salt — is so hard to resist. It is not a lack of discipline, but a physiological system responding to products that did not exist in our evolutionary history.

Environment as a determining factor

Food choices are largely determined by the environment, not by conscious decisions. Story et al. (2008) introduced the 'ecological model' of eating behaviour: individual factors (knowledge, preference, habits) are overshadowed by social and physical environmental factors such as availability, portion size, social norms and marketing.

Anyone surrounded by calorie-dense snacks at home or at work eats more of them — regardless of intention. Conversely, making fruit and vegetables available significantly increases their consumption. This means that behaviour change is more effective when it occurs at the environmental level, not only at the individual level.

Knowledge as a counterweight

Although environment and biology carry significant weight, knowledge is not irrelevant. Lieberman (2013) argues that the uniquely human capacity for cultural transmission — the ability to change behaviour based on information — is precisely the most powerful tool for coping with the evolutionary mismatch. Understanding why certain food choices are made enables the conscious construction of alternative habits.

A dietitian helps with this: not by imposing strict rules, but by providing insight into the mechanisms behind eating behaviour and jointly developing practical strategies suited to your living environment and preferences.

Practical tips

  • Eat at consistent times to limit ghrelin spikes and impulsive food choices
  • Adjust your environment: keep unhealthy snacks out of sight and put fruit on the table
  • Eat mindfully and without distractions (phone, TV) — this strengthens satiety signals
  • Understand that cravings for sweet or fatty foods are biology, not weakness — plan ahead
  • Choose varied, minimally processed foods as a base; this supports a more stable reward system

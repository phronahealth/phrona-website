What is food made of? Macro- and micronutrients

Updated: April 2026
Macronutrients Energy Micronutrients Recommendations Practical tips References

Food provides the building blocks and energy the body needs to function. Yet there is no universal dietary pattern that is optimal for everyone: genetics, activity level, health status and life stage all influence individual needs. This article covers the basics: what are macronutrients, what are micronutrients, and what do scientific guidelines say about recommended amounts?

Macronutrients

Macronutrients are the three main components of food that provide energy: carbohydrates, proteins and fats. A fourth energy source is alcohol, although it is not an essential nutrient. The Dutch Health Council (2021) establishes dietary reference values for each of these based on physiological needs and available scientific evidence.

Carbohydrates are the brain's and muscles' primary fuel. Wholegrain varieties — wholemeal bread, oats, pulses — also provide fibre that supports gut health and stabilises blood sugar. WHO/FAO (2003) recommends that at least 55–75% of total energy intake comes from carbohydrates, with emphasis on unprocessed sources.

Proteins are essential for muscle repair, cell renewal and the production of enzymes and hormones. The Health Council (2021) advises 0.83 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day for adults; athletes and older adults generally need more (1.2–1.6 g/kg). Good sources include pulses, dairy, eggs, fish and meat.

Fats are indispensable for building cell membranes, producing hormones and absorbing fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K). The type of fat matters: unsaturated fats (olive oil, nuts, oily fish) benefit cardiovascular health, while trans fats and excess saturated fats raise LDL cholesterol (EFSA, 2017).

Energy and calories

Energy intake and expenditure together determine energy balance. A positive balance (more intake than expenditure) leads to storage as glycogen or body fat. A negative balance draws on these reserves.

Total energy requirements consist of three components: basal metabolic rate (BMR, approximately 60–70%), the thermic effect of food (TEF, around 10%) and physical activity (PAL, variable). Trumbo et al. (2002) describe how energy needs vary individually based on age, sex, weight, height and activity level. Online calculators (such as the Harris-Benedict formula) provide a rough estimate; a dietitian calculates this more precisely based on your individual situation.

Calorie counting is just one approach. Nutritional quality — the density of nutrients per calorie — is at least as important as the quantity consumed.

Micronutrients

Micronutrients — vitamins and minerals — do not provide direct energy, but are essential for virtually all physiological processes: from energy production and immune function to bone formation and nerve conduction. Deficiencies are possible even with an apparently varied diet, particularly for vitamin D, iodine, iron and folate (EFSA, 2017).

Recommended amounts

The Dutch Health Council sets Recommended Daily Amounts (RDA) per age group, sex and physiological condition (pregnancy, lactation). These values represent the amount sufficient for 97.5% of the healthy population. EFSA publishes comparable Dietary Reference Values (DRVs) at the European level.

Important: RDA values are population-level norms, not personal targets. For chronic conditions, intensive sport, vegetarian or vegan eating, or specific health complaints, individual needs may differ significantly. A dietitian calculates what your body specifically requires.

Practical guidelines

  • Eat varied: variety increases the chance of getting all micro- and macronutrients
  • Choose wholegrain over refined: more fibre, vitamins and minerals per serving
  • Eat fish twice a week (including oily fish once) for omega-3 and vitamin D
  • Limit processed and red meat to a maximum of 500 grams per week
  • Use iodised salt in moderation — iodine deficiency is a real risk in the Netherlands
  • Consider vitamin D supplementation in winter, especially if you spend little time outdoors
  • Vegans: pay attention to B12, calcium, iron, zinc and omega-3 — supplementation or fortified products are necessary

Evolutionary thinking

Updated: April 2026
Nutrition Movement Sleep Stress Social connection Nature Practical tips References

Our bodies were shaped by millions of years of evolution in an environment radically different from today's world. Evolutionary thinking — also called 'Darwinian medicine' — uses this insight as a lens to understand why modern lifestyle diseases are so widespread. Gluckman and Hanson (2006) describe this as a 'mismatch': our genome is still largely calibrated for a hunter-gatherer environment, while we live in a world of supermarkets, screens and a 24-hour economy.

1. Nutrition

For most of human evolution, food was scarce and seasonal. Our brains and metabolism evolved to actively seek out and store calorie-dense foods — fat, sugar, salt. That adaptation was lifesaving in times of scarcity, but backfires in an environment of permanent abundance (Lieberman, 2013).

Ultra-processed food (UPF) amplifies this mechanism. Monteiro et al. (2019) showed that UPF accounts for an ever-growing share of caloric intake worldwide and is strongly associated with obesity, type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease. Satija et al. (2017) demonstrated that plant-based dietary patterns — rich in unprocessed products — significantly reduce the risk of chronic disease.

2. Movement

Hunter-gatherers walked an average of 9–15 kilometres per day; modern office work requires almost no physical effort. Lieberman (2013) describes prolonged sitting as an 'evolutionarily novel behaviour' for which the body was not designed. Chronic sedentary behaviour increases the risk of metabolic conditions, independently of how many hours a person exercises (Ekelund et al., 2019).

The Dutch Health Council (2015) recommends at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week, supplemented with muscle-strengthening activities. But even short breaks from sitting — standing or walking every 30 minutes — already have measurable health benefits.

3. Sleep

The circadian rhythm — our internal 24-hour clock — regulates sleep, hormone secretion, metabolism and immune function. Artificial light in the evening, irregular working hours and screen use chronically disrupt this rhythm. Czeisler (2013) describes sleep deprivation as one of the most underestimated public health issues: it raises the risk of obesity, diabetes, depression and cardiovascular conditions.

Evolutionarily, humans slept in synchrony with light: awake at sunrise, asleep after sunset. While returning fully to this pattern is not realistic, consistent sleep times, darkened bedrooms and limiting blue light in the evening already help significantly.

4. Stress

The physiological stress response — the 'fight-or-flight' system — evolved to handle acute danger. Brief stress is functional and even protective. Chronic psychosocial stress, however — work pressure, financial concerns, social isolation — continuously activates the same systems without the corresponding physical outlet (McEwen, 2007).

This leads to elevated cortisol levels, inflammatory responses and increased risks of cardiovascular disease, depression and metabolic syndrome. Evidence-based interventions include mindfulness, regular physical activity, social support and adequate sleep.

5. Social connection

Humans are ultra-social animals. Social bonding was evolutionarily essential for survival: hunting together, raising children, offering protection. Holt-Lunstad et al. (2010) analysed 148 studies and concluded that strong social relationships reduce the risk of premature death by 50% — comparable to quitting smoking.

Social loneliness is increasingly recognised as a health risk. Maintaining meaningful relationships, participating in shared activities and investing in social networks are not a luxury but a fundamental condition for health.

6. Nature and environment

Humans spent the vast majority of their evolution outdoors, in direct interaction with nature. Twohig-Bennett and Jones (2018) showed in a systematic review that exposure to green environments is associated with lower cortisol levels, lower blood pressure, less anxiety and better overall health.

While urbanisation limits this exposure, relatively simple measures are already effective: walking in parks, working near a window with a green view, or regularly spending time outside the city.

Practical application

  • Choose unprocessed, varied foods as much as possible and limit ultra-processed products
  • Break up sedentary work every 30 minutes with a short movement break
  • Set consistent sleep and wake times and limit screen use after 9 pm
  • Build a daily moment of relaxation: walking, breathing exercises or meditation
  • Actively invest in social relationships — quality matters more than quantity
  • Regularly seek out nature, even if it's just a city park

Evolutionary thinking is not a nostalgic plea for a return to the Stone Age. It is a scientific framework that helps explain why certain lifestyle choices benefit the body — and how we can account for this as best as possible in a contemporary context.

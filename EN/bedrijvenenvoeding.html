Companies and nutrition

Updated: April 2026

What we eat is not determined solely by hunger, taste or conscious choice. The food industry plays a large and often underestimated role in what ends up on our plates. By understanding how companies leverage our biology and environment, we can make more deliberate choices in the face of the decisions that confront us every day.

The rise of ultra-processed food

Monteiro et al. (2019) developed the NOVA classification, which categorises foods by degree of industrial processing. Ultra-processed food (UPF) — group 4 in this system — includes products such as soft drinks, crisps, biscuits, ready meals and many breakfast cereals. They are formulated from isolated ingredients and industrial additives, designed for maximum shelf life, palatability and profitability.

In most Western countries, UPF now accounts for over 50% of total caloric intake. Epidemiological research links high UPF consumption to obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease and higher overall mortality — independently of nutritional composition.

How the industry targets the brain

Swinburn et al. (2011) introduced the concept of the 'obesogenic environment': a physical, economic and social environment that actively promotes weight gain. The food industry contributes through pricing (calorie-dense products are relatively cheap), portion sizes (supersizing as a norm), in-store placement (eye level, checkout displays) and intensive marketing, particularly aimed at children.

Nestle (2013) extensively documented how food companies fund scientific research, strategically deploy health claims and lobby governments to limit regulation. Products are positioned as 'healthy' via selective emphasis on one positive attribute ('high in fibre', 'low fat') while overall nutritional quality remains low.

Structural versus individual solutions

The dominant discourse around overweight places responsibility on the individual: more willpower, better choices, more exercise. Swinburn et al. (2011) argue this is a fundamental misconception. When the environment systematically makes unhealthy choices easier than healthy ones, individual behaviour change is insufficient. Structural measures — taxes on sugary drinks, advertising restrictions, mandatory nutrition labelling — are more effective and more equitable.

This does not mean individual awareness is worthless. Understanding how products are engineered to promote overconsumption helps navigate an environment that is not designed with your health in mind.

Practical tips

  • Learn to read labels: focus on the ingredient list, not just the nutrition panel
  • Be critical of health claims — a product with a claim is not automatically healthy
  • Buy unprocessed products from the outer aisles of the supermarket as much as possible
  • Cook more often at home: this gives you control over ingredients, portions and preparation
  • Limit exposure to food marketing, especially for children
  • Use the NOVA classification as a guide: the less processed, the better as a foundation
